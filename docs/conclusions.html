Conclusions

Across all analyses, this project finds that financial markets are extremely difficult to predict on a day-to-day basis, but they are far from random in how risk and uncertainty evolve over time. Daily returns for assets like the S&P 500 and Bitcoin fluctuate rapidly around zero, making consistent return forecasting unreliable. However, the way volatility behaves—how turbulent or calm markets become—follows clear and persistent patterns. This distinction between unpredictable returns and structured volatility is one of the most important takeaways of the study.

Traditional equity markets and cryptocurrency markets exhibit fundamentally different risk behaviors. Equity markets such as the S&P 500 tend to absorb shocks slowly, with periods of heightened volatility lasting for extended stretches. Bitcoin, in contrast, reacts sharply to news and stress events, producing sudden volatility spikes that dissipate more quickly. This difference reflects how investors perceive and trade these assets: equities are anchored by macroeconomic fundamentals and institutional participation, while Bitcoin remains more speculative and sentiment-driven.

Cross-market interactions exist, but they are weaker than commonly assumed. While movements in volatility—especially fear as measured by the VIX—do influence equity returns, their effect on Bitcoin is limited. Likewise, Bitcoin does not meaningfully transmit return shocks back into traditional markets. These findings suggest that crypto markets primarily react to broader financial conditions rather than driving them, particularly at the daily frequency. From a risk-management perspective, this implies that Bitcoin still offers partial diversification benefits, but not insulation from market stress.

Volatility dynamics provide more actionable insight than return forecasts. Periods of high uncertainty cluster together, meaning that once markets become volatile, elevated risk tends to persist. This behavior is especially pronounced in equities, where volatility fades gradually, and less so in Bitcoin, where risk dissipates more rapidly. For investors, portfolio managers, and policymakers, this highlights that risk forecasting—not return prediction—is where models add the most value.

Advanced forecasting methods such as deep learning offer incremental improvements, but they do not overturn the fundamental limits of predictability. Neural networks produce smoother and more stable forecasts than traditional models, capturing nonlinear relationships across markets. However, they still struggle to anticipate large shocks or turning points. This reinforces a key lesson: more complex models do not eliminate uncertainty, and model choice should depend on the decision context—risk monitoring, scenario analysis, or short-horizon planning—rather than expectations of precise forecasts.

Major market events, even dramatic ones, do not necessarily create lasting structural changes. The analysis of the FTX collapse shows that while Bitcoin experienced short-term turbulence, its long-run behavior quickly reverted to normal. Average returns, trends, and distributions remained largely unchanged after the event. This suggests that crypto markets absorb shocks rapidly, and that headline events may have less persistent impact than public perception implies.

Taken together, these findings reinforce a central message for real-world decision-making: financial markets cannot be reliably predicted, but they can be meaningfully understood. While precise forecasts remain elusive, understanding how volatility behaves, how markets interact under stress, and how shocks propagate provides valuable insight for investors, regulators, and risk managers. The true value of time-series modeling lies not in predicting tomorrow’s returns, but in helping people prepare for uncertainty.

Cross-Market Return Relationships

Figure: Cross-market correlations of daily returns across Bitcoin, equities, volatility, and the U.S. dollar.

The correlation heatmap shows that traditional equity markets (S&P 500 and NASDAQ) move almost in lockstep and are strongly linked to market fear, as measured by the VIX. Bitcoin, however, exhibits much weaker correlations with all traditional assets, indicating that it does not strongly transmit or absorb daily return spillovers from equity markets. The U.S. dollar remains largely disconnected from both crypto and equity returns at the daily level.

Volatility Dynamics During Market Stress

Figure: Rolling 30-day annualized volatility shows persistent equity volatility and sharper, faster-decaying Bitcoin volatility during crisis periods.

The volatility plot highlights clear differences in how shocks propagate across markets. Equity volatility rises and remains elevated for extended periods during crises, closely tracking the VIX. Bitcoin volatility, while much higher in magnitude, reacts sharply to shocks and dissipates more quickly. This suggests that volatility spillovers are asymmetric: traditional markets experience persistent stress, while Bitcoin absorbs shocks rapidly without sustaining long-term volatility regimes.

Overview of the Modeling Framework

Figure: Overview of the modeling framework, illustrating how different methods contribute to understanding return behavior, cross-market spillovers, and volatility dynamics.

To understand how risk and information move across financial markets, the analysis uses multiple modeling approaches, each offering a different perspective. Univariate models establish that daily returns are largely unpredictable, setting a realistic baseline for what forecasting can and cannot achieve. Multivariate models then reveal how markets interact, showing that volatility and fear, rather than returns are the primary channels through which markets influence one another. Volatility models highlight how risk persists differently across assets, while deep learning models confirm that nonlinear structure exists but does not overcome fundamental uncertainty. Together, these approaches show that the value of modeling lies not in precise prediction, but in understanding how shocks, risk, and uncertainty propagate across markets.
